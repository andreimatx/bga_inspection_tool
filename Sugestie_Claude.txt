What the code is actually doing
The center logic is solid. The edge overlaps come from a weak safety net at the very end of the active grid path (the path used because use_bga_grid_roi=True). Four things combine:

Final dedup is gated off in the normal case. In _balls_from_real_candidates, _remove_duplicate_circles only runs when len(circles) > expected_slots (or pitch is None, or use_local_component_final_snap, which is off). So when the count is ≤256 — the normal case — no final dedup runs at all, and an edge double survives.
The merge distance is too tight for edge pads. _merge_pad_candidates and _remove_duplicate_circles both use duplicate_distance_factor = 0.55, i.e. ~16–19 px for r≈30–35. A parallax-elongated edge pad produces a blob centroid and a Hough center 25–40 px apart, so both survive as two circles.
snap_final_candidates_to_grid = False. Even though the grid is fit cleanly to 16×16 and assignment is one-per-slot, each kept pad is drawn at its raw (drifted) position rather than the grid slot. That drift is what makes the doubling visible and lets circles land on gaps/traces.
The overlay amplifies it. draw_pad_contour_overlay independently re-runs findContours around each ball and draws minEnclosingCircle of the best local component. Two close balls each snap to a local circle (often the same pad or an adjacent trace), and minEnclosingCircle on an elongated edge blob inflates the radius — which is exactly why your "merged" and "oversized" counts came out equal.

There's also a structural escape hatch: _regularize_final_candidates_on_roi_grid early-returns the raw support pool (not one-per-slot) when the grid fit is marginal (grid is None, or too few assignments) — which is precisely what happens on the hard boards.
